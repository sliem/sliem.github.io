Glory Unto Oilwashes - July 2020

I've always adored how magic washes felt like ever since that first coat of Agrax Earthshade (or Devlan Mud as I first knew it). Oil washes takes that magic up a notch. I've only recently started experimenting with oil washes and thus this is not a tutorial but an illustration of the glory of oil. I would recommend watching Marco Frisoni's video tutorial if you are at all intrigued after this; which what inspired me if you are at all convinced.

The power of oil is that it drys slowly. You can apply the wash and then add, subtract, and generally play around with it for hours after the first application. This gives you amazing control to achieve the effect you want. As an example, I have the dirty white of my 155th S贸l Pragmata Chimera.

There are only three steps to achieve this dirty white; basecoat the model white, slather on the oil wash, and, after an hour or so of drying, remove the excessive wash. The wash is burnt umber oil paint heavily diluted with white spirit. Both should be available in your local art supply store; a small tube of oil paint will last you a lifetime. To remove the excess wash I used white spirit-dipped cotton swabs; you will consume multiple lifetimes worth quickly.

Oils are definitely a tool I recommend to everyone. Burnt umber is simply superior to Agrax Earthshade as "talent in a bottle". 6mm Emperor's Spears Rhino - June 2020

Since last entry, I've been trying all manner of colour schemes. I painted a very ugly yellow Rhino, a very good pink and tried out an all metallic Doom Eagles. None really clicked; the yellow was ugly, the pink was way too much effort and the metallic silver looks flat in 6mm scale. I came back to the fact that blue is a pretty good colour. As I didn't want to do Ultramarines but among the off-brand blue Chapters I found the Emperor's Spears:

The blue might be a smidge too dark for Emperor's Spears, but it's straight out of a spray can which is a huge plus. (Sadly, I don't yet have the space for an airbrush setup.) The steps are Vallejo Magic Blue primer, some sparse edge highlighting, white and grey details followed by oil washes (phthalo blue and lamp black). Using oil rather than acrylic washes really helps on such angular models as vehicles. You can easily undo mistakes and preserve that clean surface.

Overall I'm quite happy with it. It's a cleaner look my usual blanchitsu-esque style which this scale demand, and I can pump-out nine Rhinos easily. Next steps are to read the Emperor's Spears book and paint some tactical marines.

6mm Crimson Fists Test Rhino - June 2020

The next step in my 6mm adventure is to figure out how to paint my tiny marines. The Crimson Fists are as old school as they come which seemed fitting with an old school game. So I painted up a Rhino in their dark blue.

I'm not convinced yet, the dark blue might just be too dark blue, and the black oil wash isn't doing it any favours in that regard. Without the stark wash, however, there wasn't much depth to the figure. Maybe I should consider a brighter coloured chapter? Doom Eagles?

Starting Epic - June 2020

Recently I've caught a 6mm bug. I've been playing Epic: Armageddon, specifically the community-supported NetEA variant, and have enjoyed it immensely. The small scale and the abstracted ruleset allows for a sense of tactical play it's bigger brother 40k seldom did.

Until now I've been playing with carton board proxies but I went a bit excessive and ordered figures from third parties such as Vanguard and Trollz as well as bidding on eBay auctions. From one such auction, I scored ten old-school Land Raiders and nine rhinos for less than the shipping cost. I'm currently in the process of stripping them (someone did not use two thin coats) and agonizing over the colour scheme. At the very moment I'm contemplating the yellow of the Marines Malevolent as I got a magenta oil wash I'm itching to use.

I'll keep you updated!
